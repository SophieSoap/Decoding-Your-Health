Decoding Your Health

Understanding The Quantified Self Through Samsung Health

Health tracking apps like Samsung Health promote the Quantified Self movement by shifting health monitoring from intuition to data-driven insights. This project critically examines Samsung Health's features and visualisations, exploring its impact on health awareness and questioning the necessity and influence of extensive personal data.

Introduction

Health tracking apps are on the rise, offering much more than just step counting. They enable users to monitor sleep patterns, track exercises and log in additional health-related information. These tools have transformed how individuals engage with their well-being, shifting the focus from intuition to quantifiable data - a hallmark of the Quantified Self movement.

Apps like Samsung Health play a significant role in this transformation by collecting and visualising vast amounts of personal health data. But how does Samsung Health make this information understandable for the user? What happens to the data you track and how often do you actually reflect on it? Despite the convenience and insights these tools offer, very few of us take a closer look at the data we've accumulated over days, weeks, or months.

This project takes a critical approach to analysing self-tracking technologies through the lens of the Samsung Health app. By deconstructing its features and visualisations, we aim to explore its impact on the Quantified Self movement and address broader questions:

Do you truly need all this data? And how does it influence your relationship with your own health?

The Rise of Self-Tracking

Self-tracking devices exemplify a shift toward increased health awareness, enabling users to track metrics such as physical activity, sleep patterns, and overall wellness.

The rise of self-tracking has been partly driven by social media platforms like Instagram and TikTok, which have popularised fitness challenges, making health journeys more engaging, and increased consciousness of preventive healthcare, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic.

The evolution of self-tracking began with the first commercial pedometer “Manpo-Kei” in 1965. To market the “Manpo-meter” they established a 10.000-steps-a-day-rule, a claim that is not backed by science and was only made up as a marketing strategy. The current recommendation by the WHO is 8.000 steps a day for adults.

Between 2008 and 2015 several companies launched fitness trackers as well as health and fitness apps, including Fitbit (2008), Strava (2012) and Apple Health (2015). Samsung launched its own fitness app, Samsung Health, in 2012.

Since 2016, the number of users has more than doubled, from 150 million to 368 million.

Revenue from the fitness tracker market has grown nearly fivefold, from $0.77 billion in 2016 to $3.58 billion in 2023.

Samsung holds about 10% of the wearable-tracker market share, trailing behind Apple, which makes it the second largest manufacturer of smart watches and fitness trackers.

In the App-market, Samsung's position is much less prominent. Samsung Health is free and doesn’t contain ads as it's likely generating their revenue. through partnerships and the potential use of user data for internal development and third-party sales. Nonetheless Samsung Health is still a common fitness app with a user base of around 64 Million Users worldwide.

The Quantified Self

The Quantified Self movement defines the concept of self-tracking and quantifying everyday life. By integrating wearable devices and apps into daily routines, it aims to provide individuals with tools to enhance their improve physical, mental and emotional well-being – an approach often referred to as “self-knowledge through numbers”. These technologies promise actionable insights and greater self-awareness, but the movement is not without its critics.

Supporters of the Quantified Self highlight how tracking data offers users new perspectives on health management, from understanding sleep patterns to improving dietary habits. However, the movement has faced criticism, with some arguing that it places too much emphasis on data at the expense of personal intuition that can lead to obsessive behaviours. Additionally, there are concerns about the broader societal implications, such as the potential misuse of data and the emergence of “datacracy” – a dependency on and reduction of human experiences to numerical values.

To make self-tracking more engaging and accessible, many applications utilise user-friendly design features, like gamification, animations, and micro-interactions.
