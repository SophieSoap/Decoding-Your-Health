Conclusion

The Samsung Health App showcases the potential of self-tracking technologies through its user-centric design, motivational features and intuitive visualisations, it empowers individuals to monitor and improve their health in ways that were unimaginable just a decade ago. However, this potential comes with significant trade-offs that merit critical reflection.

While Samsung Health and many other health apps appear free to use, they often come at the cost of user data which can be analysed, monetised, and shared with third parties, such as advertisers, insurers, or other institutions. The promise of better health and personalised insights often involves the commodification of personal health data.

Beyond data concerns, the focus on metrics can foster obsession, undermine intuition, and encourage unhealthy behaviours, promoting a reductive view of health disconnected from personal experience.

While self-tracking can undoubtedly empower individuals to take control of their health, it also raises an essential question: Who truly benefits? Are these tools genuinely designed for the user’s well-being, or do they primarily serve capitalistic corporate interests?

As we embrace the tools of the Quantified Self movement, it is vital to critically evaluate their broader implications. At what cost do we quantify the self?
